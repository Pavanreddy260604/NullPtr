      c. Explain the major transport layer issues and TCP Impairments in DCNs.

Transport Layer Issues in DCNs

Dominance of TCP

Ø TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) is one of the most widely used transport protocols on the Internet.

Ø It handles a large variety of Internet applications and forms most of the traffic in both types of Data Center Networks (DCNs).

Ø It has been the workhorse of the Internet since its inception.

Role and Success of TCP

Ø The success of the Internet is partly due to the congestion control mechanisms built into TCP.

Ø As the Internet’s scale and usage have grown exponentially, TCP has evolved to remain scalable and robust.

Limitations of TCP in DCNs

Ø Despite its strengths, state-of-the-art TCP fails to meet the three key DCN performance requirements.
These issues arise due to several impairments, including:

Ø TCP Incast

Ø TCP Outcast

Ø Queue buildup

Ø Buffer pressure

Ø Pseudocongestion effect


 

TCP Impairments in DCNs

Evolution of TCP

Ø TCP has evolved over the past three decades to adapt to changing network conditions.

Challenges in Modern Networks

Ø The diversity of current and next-generation network characteristics and varied application requirements have created new challenges for TCP’s congestion control mechanisms.

TCP Incast

Ø TCP incast is a pathological behavior of TCP that leads to underutilization of link capacity in many-to-one communication patterns.

Ø Common in partition/aggregate applications such as web search, MapReduce, social networks, and advertisement selection.

Occurrence in Data Centers

Ø TCP incast is widely present in distributed storage systems, data-intensive scalable computing, and partition/aggregate workflows.

Many-to-One Communication Pattern

Ø An aggregator sends requests to multiple worker nodes.

Ø All worker nodes reply simultaneously, causing data to converge on a bottleneck link.

Ø This leads to buffer overflow at top-of-the-rack (ToR) switches, resulting in packet loss — a phenomenon called synchronized mice collision.

Barrier Synchronized Transmission

Ø Worker nodes cannot send the next data block until all finish sending the current block — known as barrier synchronized transmission.

Impact on Performance

Ø As the number of worker nodes increases, application throughput decreases due to packet losses.

Ø Retransmissions occur only after a retransmit timeout (RTO), typically a few milliseconds, harming mice traffic, which requires short response times.

Ø Fast retransmit mechanisms are ineffective for mice traffic because of the small number of packets and insufficient duplicate acknowledgements (dupacks).

Mitigation of TCP Incast

Ø Solutions exist at multiple layers — application, transport, and link layers.

Ø Some approaches propose modifying TCP, others replacing it, and some use non-transport layer techniques.

TCP Outcast

Ø TCP Outcast occurs when a small set of flows and a large set of flows arriving at different input ports of a switch compete for the same bottleneck output port.

Ø The small set of flows suffers a significant drop in throughput.

Cause of TCP Outcast

Ø Mainly occurs in data center switches that use drop-tail queues.

Ø Drop-tail queues cause consecutive packet drops from one port, leading to frequent TCP timeouts — this effect is known as port blackout.

Impact of Port Blackout

Ø Frequent timeouts increase latency and degrade response times for small flows.

Ø Example: In a switch where ports A and B compete for output port C, packets from one port (e.g., A) are dropped consecutively, while packets from the other (B) are successfully queued.

RTT Bias and Inverse RTT Bias

Ø Normally, TCP throughput is inversely proportional to the Round-Trip Time (RTT) flows with lower RTT get higher bandwidth.

Ø In DCNs affected by TCP Outcast, the opposite happens: flows with low RTT are outcasted by those with high RTT — known as inverse RTT bias.

Ø Main Factors Causing TCP Outcast

(1) Use of drop-tail queues in switches.

(2) Many-to-one communication patterns, where multiple flows converge on a single output port.

Ø Both are common in data center networks (DCNs).

Mitigation of TCP Outcast

Ø Replace drop-tail queues with other queuing mechanisms such as:

o   Random Early Detection (RED)

o   Stochastic Fair Queue (SFQ)

Ø Alternatively, reduce buffer occupancy by designing efficient TCP congestion control laws at the end hosts.

Ø Example scenario of port blackout

Queue Buildup

Ø In Data Center Networks (DCNs), mice traffic, cat traffic, and elephant traffic coexist.

Ø The long-lasting and greedy nature of elephant traffic can cause extreme congestion and buffer overflows at bottlenecks.

Ø When mice traffic and elephant traffic share the same route, the performance of mice traffic is severely impacted.

Effects on Mice Traffic

Ø The performance degradation of mice traffic occurs in two ways:

o   Packet Losses:

§  Elephant traffic occupies most of the buffer space.

§  Mice traffic packets are more likely to be dropped, leading to frequent timeouts similar to the TCP Incast problem.

o   Increased Queuing Delay:

§  Even without packet loss, mice traffic packets experience high latency as they wait behind elephant traffic packets.

§  This condition is known as queue buildup.

Mitigating Queue Buildup

Ø The problem can be mitigated by minimizing queue occupancy in DCN switches.

Ø Traditional TCP variants use a reactive approach to congestion control (reducing sending rate only after packet loss).

Ø A proactive congestion control approach is needed to minimize queue buildup and maintain performance.

Buffer Pressure

Ø Buffer pressure is caused by the long-lasting and greedy nature of elephant traffic.

Ø When mice traffic and elephant traffic share the same route, the elephant traffic occupies most of the buffer space.

Impact on Mice Traffic

Ø With limited buffer space available, bursts of mice traffic packets (common in many-to-one communication) cannot be accommodated.

Ø This leads to packet loss and poor performance of mice traffic.

Ø Since most DCN traffic is bursty, mice packets are frequently dropped because elephant flows persist longer and continue to occupy buffer space.

Mitigation of Buffer Pressure - Similar to queue buildup, the solution lies in minimizing buffer occupancy in switches.

Pseudo congestion Effect

Role of Virtualization

Ø Virtualization is a key technology enabling cloud computing by using Virtual Machines (VMs) to provide on-demand services and remote access.

Ø Data centers that use VMs are called virtualized data centers.

Advantages of Virtualization

Ø Provides efficient server utilization, service isolation, and lower system maintenance cost.

Ø However, it also impacts the performance of traditional protocols like TCP and UDP.

Performance Degradation

Ø Studies (e.g., Amazon EC2) show that virtualization causes unstable throughput and high end-to-end delay, even under low network load.

Ø The main cause is hypervisor scheduling latency — the delay each VM experiences while waiting for CPU access.

Hypervisor Scheduling Latency

Ø Increases as more VMs share the same physical machine.

Ø Can range from microseconds to several hundred milliseconds.

Ø Leads to unpredictable network delays (RTT), unstable throughput, and longer end-to-end delay.

Ø High latency may trigger Retransmission Timeout (RTO) at the VM sender.

Definition of Pseudo congestion

Ø When RTO occurs due to hypervisor latency, the VM sender mistakenly assumes network congestion and reduces the sending rate.

Ø This false detection is called the pseudo congestion effect, since the congestion is not real but caused by virtualization delays.

Mitigation of Pseudo congestion

Ø Two main approaches to address the issue:

o   Design efficient hypervisor schedulers to minimize scheduling latency.

o   Modify TCP to intelligently detect pseudo congestion and react appropriately.

     c.    Explain how cooling infrastructure is managed in modern data centers.

Cooling Infrastructure in Modern Data Centers

Modern data centers house thousands of servers that generate significant heat, requiring sophisticated cooling systems to maintain optimal operating temperatures and ensure energy efficiency.

1. Raised Floor and Aisle Arrangement

  • Servers are placed on a raised plenum (floor) allowing cold air to circulate underneath.
  • Server racks are arranged in alternating front-facing (cold aisles) and back-facing (hot aisles) rows.
  • Cold air is pushed up through perforated tiles in the cold aisles, and server fans draw this air through the equipment.
  • The hot air exiting from the back rises into the hot aisles and is directed to the chiller plant for cooling and recirculation.
  • This design forms the hot aisle–cold aisle layout, improving airflow management and cooling efficiency.

2. Air Conditioning and Chiller Systems

  • Building-level air-conditioning units and chiller plants cool the warm air collected from the hot aisles.
  • Fans and ducts help in even distribution of cooled air.
  • Deflectors or containment panels are used to prevent mixing of hot and cold air, reducing hot spots.

3. Advanced Cooling Innovations

  • Recent designs include liquid cooling, immersion cooling, and rear-door heat exchangers for high-density racks.
  • Free cooling techniques use outside air or evaporative cooling to reduce power consumption.
  • Green data center designs use energy-efficient materials and renewable energy to minimize environmental impact.

4. Environmental and Design Factors

  • Site selection considers climate, power availability, and disaster risk.
  • Some companies like Google, Microsoft, and Yahoo build hyper scale facilities that integrate power and cooling innovations at massive scale.
  • Data centers are now designed as modular, mobile, or hybrid structures that can optimize cooling and power efficiency dynamically.

5. Intelligent Monitoring and Management

  • Modern DCs use sensors, AI, and automated control systems to monitor temperature, humidity, and energy usage.
  • Dynamic resource allocation and real-time adjustments help maintain ideal cooling with minimal energy waste.

     d.    Explain in detail the four types of network accesses required in data centers.

Types of Network Accesses Required in Data Centers

Modern Data Centers (DCs) require multiple network types to handle communication between clients, servers, storage systems, and management components.

The four major types of network accesses are:

1. Client–Server Network

  • Purpose: Provides external connectivity between the data center and end users or clients on the Internet.
  • Technology Used: Traditional wired Ethernet or Wireless LAN (Wi-Fi).
  • Function:
    • Clients send requests to applications hosted in the data center.
    • The responses are sent back over the same network.
  • Example: A user accessing a web application or cloud service hosted in the data center.
  • Requirement: High reliability, strong security, and stable Internet connectivity.

2. Server–Server Network

  • Purpose: Enables high-speed communication among servers inside the data center.
  • Technology Used: Ethernet, InfiniBand (IBA), or similar high-speed interconnects.
  • Function:
    • Allows distributed servers to exchange data efficiently.
    • Supports load balancing and parallel processing for applications running on multiple servers.
  • Requirement: Low latency, high throughput, and fault tolerance.
  • Example: Communication between an application server and a database server within the same data center.

3. Server–Storage Network

  • Purpose: Provides fast and reliable connectivity between servers and storage systems.
  • Technology Used: Commonly Fibre Channel, though Ethernet and InfiniBand can also be used.
  • Function:
    • Facilitates data storage, retrieval, and backup operations.
    • Ensures that data is always available to compute nodes.
  • Requirement: High bandwidth, low latency, and high data integrity.
  • Example: File transfers between storage arrays and application servers.

4. Management Network

  • Purpose: Used to monitor, manage, and control data center infrastructure.
  • Technology Used: Typically Ethernet, often with separate cabling from production networks.
  • Function:
    • Handles administrative tasks such as configuration, updates, and health monitoring.
    • Isolates management traffic from regular data traffic for security and performance.
  • Requirement: Secure access, reliability, and redundancy.
  • Example: Network used for controlling switches, routers, and virtualization management tools.
